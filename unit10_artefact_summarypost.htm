Collaborative Discussion 2: Comparing Compliance Laws - Summary Post

My initial post compared the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the Japanâ€™s Act on the Protection of Personal Information (APPI).

Both the GDPR and the APPI share the fundamental objective of safeguarding individuals' personal data and exhibit certain similarities and differences in their principles, rights and enforcement mechanisms. Both laws establish guidelines for the lawful collection, use, processing and storage of personal information. They also grant data subjects specific rights. Furthermore, data security measures must be implemented by organisations to prevent unauthorised access, disclosure or destruction of personal data. Consent from data subjects is also a shared requirement in both the GDPR and the APPI for data collection and processing.

Notable differences between the two laws were also examined in the Initial Post. The GDPR specifies specific legal bases for lawful data processing, including consent, contract performance, legal obligations, vital interests, public task and legitimate interests. In contrast, the APPI does not outline legal bases as explicitly as the GDPR.

An essential distinction lies in the scope of applicability. While the GDPR does not pertain to anonymised data, the APPI does, even if re-identification of data subjects is not possible.

The implementation of data security measures such as anonymisation is of utmost importance; however, it also entails considerable implications concerning the trade-off between levels of data protection and data utility. Prioritising considerations of re-identification risks, however, should remain a key focus, when collecting and processing personal data for business or research purposes which must be firmly supported with lawful bases.

Comparing these legal frameworks emphasises the universal concept of the relationship between humans and machines, despite the local variations in laws. Such analysis sheds light on the human-centric considerations of human dignity concerning personal data (Miyashita, 2021).

Reference

Miyashita, H. (2021) Human-centric data protection laws and policies: A lesson from Japan. Computer Law & Security Review, 40, 105487. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clsr.2020.105487 [Accessed 21 July 2023].
